2. Desire (Kāma)

Rock 1All indecision produces desires; it is called the root of desire; suppress indecision and (desire) will arise in thee no more.

Rock 2From desires comes grief, from desires comes fear; he who is free from desires knows neither grief nor fear.

Rock 3From pleasures comes grief, from pleasures comes fear; he who is free from pleasures knows neither grief nor fear.

Rock 4The fruit of desires and pleasures ripens into sorrow; their at first agreeable fruit is burning, as the torch that has not been cast away does finally burn the fool.

Rock 5Look at those who are fondly attached to jewels, earrings, to their children (those are fetters); but iron, wood, and rope make not strong fetters, says the Blessed One.

Rock 6It is hard for one who is held by the fetters of desire to free himself of them, says the Blessed One. The steadfast, who care not for the happiness of desires, cast them off, and do soon depart (to nirvāna).

Rock 7There is no being in the world who is not through his indecision affected by desires, yet they who are steadfast seek to free themselves of desires, though they do pervade the world.

Rock 8Mankind has no lasting desires; they are impermanent in them who experience them: free yourselves then from what cannot last, and abide not in the sojourn of death.

Rock 9The sinless and reflective mind in which a desire arises experiences no misery from it; the different desires disturb it not: he who (has such a mind) is beyond death, I declare.

Rock 10As the smith does with the silver, so does the intelligent man, gradually and little by little, cleanse himself of all his impurities.

Rock 11As the shoemaker, when he has well prepared his leather, can use it to make shoes, so when one has cast off desires, he has the highest happiness.

Rock 12If one longs for happiness, let him cast off all desires; he who has cast off all desires will find the most perfect happiness.

Rock 13As long as one follows after desire, one finds no satisfaction; they who through wisdom have given it up find contentment.

Rock 14Desires are never satiated; wisdom affords contentment: he who has the contentment of wisdom cannot fall into the power of lust.

Rock 15They who have fondness for pleasure, and who delight only in what is wrong, would not perceive the danger they run, even if their life were drawing to a close.

Rock 16The evil-minded is subdued by wealth and seeks not after the other world; his mind is subverted by his fondness for desires; he brings destruction on himself and on others.

Rock 17Even a shower of Karśapanas would not satisfy the covetous; the wise know full well that desires bring little contentment and (much) pain.

Rock 18Not even in the pleasures of the gods does the disciple of the perfect Buddha find pleasure; he rejoices only in the destruction of desires.

Rock 19Even a mountain of riches like unto Himavat would not suffice for the wealth of a single man; he who has understanding knows this full well.

Rock 20They who know that this (i.e., desire) is the origin of sorrow, how can they delight in pleasures? Having learnt that this is the cause of pain in the world, they acquire steadfastness to help to control themselves.

Chapter on Desire, the Second.
