5. Agreeable Things

Rock 1From those things that are agreeable comes sorrow; from those things that are agreeable comes fear: if one casts off agreeable things he will be without sorrow, without fear.

Rock 2From that which is enjoyable comes fear; from the agreeable comes misery, from the agreeable fear: if that which is pretty changes, one reaps but despair.

Rock 3The miseries of the world are numerous—sorrow, lamentations, cries, etc.; they all arise from holding on to those things that are agreeable: if one gives up what is agreeable they will all cease to be.

Rock 4They for whom there is nothing whatever agreeable in the world are happy and without sorrow; they therefore who would be without affliction, free from human passions, must never do that which is agreeable.

Rock 5Not to see what is agreeable is painful, so likewise is the sight of what is not agreeable; one must never seek what is agreeable; he must not seek what is not agreeable.

Rock 6It is by being deprived of what is agreeable and by finding what is disagreeable that men create (for themselves) the intolerable sorrow of age.

Rock 7When that in which one delights does die, such as one's kinsfolk or friends, it brings one a great and enduring sorrow, for to be separated from that which brings pleasure is painful.

Rock 8He who knows neither agreeable or disagreeable is without bonds; he, therefore, who considers the agreeable as sinful will give up what is agreeable.

Rock 9He who, having ceased to consider what is agreeable, has nought to do with attachment to (worldly) happiness, who has attachment to the happiness that is not that of the individual, seeks the object of his fond desires (nirvāṇa).

Rock 10He who among gods and men is held by fondness for what is pleasing in the body (rūpa), does evil and suffers affliction, he falls into the power of age and death.

Rock 11He who is steadfast both by day and night, who casts away what is pleasing in the body (rūpa), which is difficult to do, pulls up by the very roots sin, that food of Māra.

Rock 12The foolish people, who consider what is not good as good, what is not agreeable as agreeable, what is misery as happiness, will surely come to destruction.

Rock 13He who, doing evil, would be made happy by it, finds no contentment, let him not do evil.

Rock 14He who, doing what is right, would be made happy by it, finds contentment, let him not do evil.

Rock 15As the frontier town is protected by strong retrenchments, so let him who would be happy protect himself by strong defences.

Rock 16The wise man who would be made happy watches during all the three watches; his watchfulness makes him safe.

Rock 17When the frontier town is well guarded within and without, its peace is not disturbed: do likewise and watch thyself; for when one has been born in hell his peace is gone and he repents him (of what he has left undone).

Rock 18Look where you will, there is nothing dearer to man than himself; therefore, as it is the same thing that is dear to you and to others, hurt not others with what pains yourself.

Rock 20To all men this life is dear; all men fear punishment; you, who are like unto them, strike not, put not to death.

Rock 21He who has been to a great distance and who returns from afar without mishap, his assembled kinsfolk and friends receive him with joyful cries of “Alala!”

Rock 22So likewise he who has been virtuous, on arriving from this world into another, his good works receive him like kinsfolk and welcome him.

Rock 23Lay up, therefore, good works in view of the other world; for it is good works that receive beings in the other world.

Rock 24He whose life is one of virtue is praised by the gods; he in whom there is nothing to be blamed finds perfect joy in heaven.

Rock 25He who observes the law, who is perfectly virtuous, modest, speaking the truth, doing what he ought to do, delights the rest of mankind.

Rock 26He who, doing what he ought to do, and who, extolling the true law, gives to others pleasure, shall find joy in the other world.

Rock 27His speech is edifying, and he has given up all wrong doings, in this delights the righteous, and delights not the unrighteous.

Rock 28Therefore what is good and what is not good are separated at death; the unrighteous go to hell, the righteous go to heaven.

Chapter on Pleasure, the Fifth.
